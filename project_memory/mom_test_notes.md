# Mom Test Notes & Transcripts

This document contains simulated interviews based on the NestSync personas, applying The Mom Test methodology (focusing on past behaviors, uncovering real problems, and avoiding feature pitches).

## User 1: Maya (The Overwhelmed College Student)
*Date:* February 27, 2026
*Goal:* Understand how roommates currently handle chores and authority without a formal system.

### Simulated Transcript
**Interviewer:** Hey Maya, how are things going at the new apartment with the roommates?
**Maya:** Okay overall, but it's getting kinda messy. No one wants to be the bad guy and tell people to clean, so things just pile up.
**Interviewer:** Talk me through the last time someone tried to get the apartment cleaned.
**Maya:** Last week, Sarah sent this passive-aggressive text to the group chat about the sink being full of dishes. We all just ignored it for two days because she's always complaining. Eventually, she just did it herself and was super mad. 
**Interviewer:** What happened after she did the dishes?
**Maya:** She called a "house meeting" but two people "forgot" and didn't show up. It just feels like no one is actually in charge, so no one cares to listen.
**Interviewer:** Have you guys tried anything to fix this organization issue?
**Maya:** We tried a whiteboard chore chart on the fridge, but people just stopped checking off their names. It's too easy to ignore.
**Interviewer:** Why do you think they ignore the whiteboard?
**Maya:** Because there's no consequence! If Sarah complains, she's just being annoying. If we had an actual "house manager" maybe it would be different, but no one wants to nominate themselves to be the boss of their friends.

### Mom Test Notes
- `^` Lives with 3 roommates, no clear authority structure.
- `☇` Chores are ignored; conflicts are handled via passive-aggressive texts in group chats.
- `☐` Whiteboard chore chart failed because it's easy to ignore and lacks consequences.
- `⚽` Wants clear accountability without ruining friendships or sounding bossy.
- `⤴` Sarah ends up doing the work resentfully.
- `☑` Implied need: A source of legitimate authority or rotation that isn't just one roommate appointing themselves.

## User 2: David (The Working Professional)
*Date:* February 27, 2026
*Goal:* Understand how shared expenses and utility bills are handled and where friction occurs.

### Simulated Transcript
**Interviewer:** Hey David, in your 3-person apartment, how do you guys handle the shared bills?
**David:** I usually pay the internet and utilities, and my roommate Tom pays for the shared groceries. We just sort of Venmo each other at the end of the month.
**Interviewer:** Walk me through how that end-of-month settling up went last month.
**David:** Man, it was a headache. I had to scroll through my credit card statement to find the exact utility amounts, then I put the totals in our group text. But Tom forgot to include the toilet paper and paper towels he bought. Then our third roommate, Alex, took five days to pay me back because the group chat got buried by a bunch of TikTok links.
**Interviewer:** Why didn't you use an app specifically for splitting bills, like Splitwise?
**David:** We tried it last year! But Alex hated it. He said it felt "too transactional," like we were constantly nickel-and-diming each other for every $3 coffee or Uber ride. So we went back to just "vibing it" at the end of the month, which honestly means I probably lose like $20 to $30 a month just from forgetting small stuff.
**Interviewer:** You lose $20 a month? How do you feel about that?
**David:** It's annoying, but $20 isn't worth starting a fight over with guys I have to live with. I just wish there was a way to keep a running tab that didn't feel so aggressive, you know?

### Mom Test Notes
- `^` 3 professional roommates; David handles core utilities.
- `☇` Financial requests get lost in the noise of social group chats. Awkward to chase people for money.
- `☐` Splitwise felt "too transactional" and caused friendship friction.
- `⤴` "Vibing it" at the end of the month via Venmo, resulting in David losing money.
- `＄` Willing to absorb $20-$30/month loss to avoid conflict with roommates.
- `⚽` Wants a fair, visible running balance that doesn't feel aggressive or nickel-and-diming.

## User 3: Linda (The Busy Parent)
*Date:* February 27, 2026
*Goal:* Understand how chores and announcements are managed within a family unit.

### Simulated Transcript
**Interviewer:** Hi Linda, how has household management been lately with the kids?
**Linda:** Exhausting, honestly. I feel like I'm constantly nagging everyone to take out the trash or feed the dog.
**Interviewer:** Tell me about yesterday. What happened when the trash needed to go out?
**Linda:** I asked Leo, my 14-year-old, to do it before dinner. He said "in a minute" without looking away from his game. An hour later, dinner's ready, and the trash is still sitting there. I ended up yelling at him, he got super defensive, and it ruined the mood for the whole dinner.
**Interviewer:** Have you tried any systems to manage these chores better before?
**Linda:** We tried allowance-tied chore lists on the fridge a few months ago. It worked perfectly for a week. Then I got busy at work, forgot to check the list, and they realized they could get away with not doing them because I wasn't auditing it. I work full time; I can't be a project manager at home too.
**Interviewer:** What tools do you use for your job when managing projects?
**Linda:** Oh, Jira, Asana, Slack. But I refuse to use those at home! That feels way too corporate and stressful. Home should be home.
**Interviewer:** What would the ideal scenario look like when you ask Leo to do the trash?
**Linda:** Ideally, I wouldn't have to ask at all! It would just be his standard responsibility, and if he didn't do it, there would be a clear, objective record so I don't sound "crazy" when I point out he hasn't done it all week. I want the system to be the bad guy, not me.

### Mom Test Notes
- `^` Working parent, 2 kids, currently the default household manager.
- `☇` Constant nagging leads to arguments and ruins family time (emotional pain point).
- `☐` Physical fridge lists fail because parents forget to enforce them (requires active management).
- `☐` Refuses to use workplace tools (Jira/Asana) at home because they feel "corporate" and stressful.
- `⤴` Ends up tracking chores in her head, yelling, or doing it herself.
- `⚽` Wants kids to take ownership of tasks with a visible tracker, so she doesn't have to be the "bad guy" nagging them.
